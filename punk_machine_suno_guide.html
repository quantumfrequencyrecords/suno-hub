Blink-182's sonic DNA: Mark Hoppus and Tom DeLonge alternate lead vocal on most tracks — a vocal tag team where some songs belong to Tom, some to Mark, and key tracks like "Feeling This" and "I Miss You" alternate them. Travis Barker's drumming is the engine — aggressive, precise, fills everywhere. The band spans from their classic pop-punk era through their self-titled mature album, which used vintage gear and experimental analog techniques recorded in a rented mansion converted into a musical laboratory. Their 2023 reunion album brought Tom back and fused their classic sound with modern production.

Green Day's sonic DNA: Their sound is described as "often hard-driving and aggressive" with heavy use of power chords combined with "strong pop melodic sensibility." Producer Rob Cavallo shaped their major-label sound from Dookie through American Idiot. American Idiot — their "punk rock opera" — follows a fictional anti-hero named Jesus of Suburbia and expresses the disillusionment of a generation shaped by 9/11 and the Iraq War. Billie Joe Armstrong is one of rock's most efficient songwriters: three chords, maximum impact, every word earning its place.

RATM's sonic DNA: Recorded without a click track — all albums have natural tempo drift. The mean tempo is around 98 BPM, the true mode is 84–90 BPM. Most songs are slow, which is a big part of their distinctive heavy sound. Tom Morello identifies as a DJ using his guitar — using the kill switch rhythmically (as on "Bulls on Parade"), imitating record scratching, and employing a DigiTech Whammy pedal that he credits with opening "very new sonic possibilities." Zack de la Rocha's vocal mode combines rap verses with screamed slogans.

Important Suno note: RATM's sound pushes against Suno's content filters more than any other band in this set — political content and de la Rocha-style sloganeering may require framing as anti-authoritarian rather than explicitly radical. The lyric prompts below are calibrated for this.
